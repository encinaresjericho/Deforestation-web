Frequently Asked Questions

Deforestation can have significant negative impacts on the environment, including loss of biodiversity, disruption of ecosystems, soil erosion, and contribution to climate change through the release of greenhouse gases.

Forests are home to a wide variety of plant and animal species. When forests are cleared, these species lose their habitats, leading to a loss of biodiversity. Many species may become endangered or extinct as a result.

The main drivers of deforestation include agriculture (such as clearing land for cattle ranching and crop cultivation), logging (for timber and paper products), urban expansion, infrastructure development, and mining.

Trees absorb carbon dioxide (CO2) from the atmosphere during photosynthesis and store it in their biomass. When trees are cut down or burned, this stored carbon is released back into the atmosphere as CO2, contributing to the greenhouse effect and climate change.

Solutions to deforestation include sustainable forest management practices, reforestation and afforestation efforts, promoting alternative livelihoods for communities dependent on forest resources, enforcing laws and regulations against illegal logging and land clearance, and supporting conservation initiatives.

International agreements and initiatives such as the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) and the REDD+ (Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation) program aim to reduce deforestation and promote sustainable forest management worldwide.
